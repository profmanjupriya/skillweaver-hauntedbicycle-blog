The Great Migration

It wasn't actually that great, we just moved our services off of DigitalOcean.

Jesus Hernandez July 20, 2026 ~10 min read
In this post
  1. Preperation
  2. Migration
  3. Dockerization

During the first rush of development, we used to host our app in DigitalOcean. Is there anything inheritently wrong with that? No, of course not, we just quickly outgrew our small Droplet.

Our initial configuration consisted of one singular shared CPU core, two whole Gigabytes of RAM, and a measly 20 GB of sweet, sweet storage. While this might sound plentiful for a simple web application, especially for basic testing, we quickly realized that we would eventually outgrow this. Not only would we want to want to be able to have the capacity to serve many users at once, but we would also want to add many improvements to our project, including data analysis and the heavy, unforgiving use of dockerization.

Preperation

Naturally, we asked ourselves where we'd even migrate to, since there's so many options out there. Well, I sure am glad you (hadn't) asked! At the time, our requirements mainly consisted of having enough headroom to be able to experiment with any piece of software that we'd like, and having a reasonable enough uptime for development and testing purposes. No sort of Tier 4 SLA or fault redundancy was strictly necessary, as a short outage wouldn't really cause us any harm at all. So, we soon decided to own our own hardware, and set it up as a dedicated server! We now have eight whole dedicated CPU cores based on Zen 3, 32 GB of RAM, and a huge amount of storage at our disposal.

Lastly, before we migrated anything to that new server, we had to document everything that we needed to migrate over. Some things were quite obvious, like the source code for Skill Weaver, and the existing database. Other things were less obvious, such as login credentials, and how we'd manage external access to the server. We ended up using Tailscale for its tunneling service to access our machine, and also took note of everything that needed to be moved over.

Migration

So, it was finally time to migrate! The very first step I took during this process was to take a backup of what already existed - we don't want to lose any of our existing work, should we ever need it! After installing the operating system onto our shiny new server, we started migrating all of our necessary files, including any relevant configuration that we were using. Our main goal with this was to get a 1 to 1 replica of what we had on our Droplet working on our dedicated server.

And, in classic fashion, it *almost* worked first try! The web application worked, but since our database had been configured to run on its own, even smaller Droplet, it needed to be reconfigured entirely. Thankfully, setting up external access was pretty smooth sailing, though. It honestly felt quite absurd seeing such an overkill computer running a program with such a small footprint, it was like driving a semi truck in order to ship a singular bicycle, which may or may not be haunted.

Dockerization

Yep, you read that right. Up to this point, we had been running our project on the bare operating system, and it didn't even have a certificate for SSL! As such, my first move after migrating off of our Droplet was to put everything we serve under Docker, and have an NGINX container that accepts HTTPS requests and acts as a reverse proxy to our web app.

But, especially with how there have been a couple high profile container escape exploits recently, I didn't want us to take any chances. I opted in to setting up Docker in rootless mode, which essentially means that its daemon runs as a regular user, and if any escape were to occur, the intruder would still be a regular user rather than having any sort of free will as root. Of course, this doesn't mean our system is bulletproof, as no system is nor will ever be bulletproof, but it is at least a bit more resilient.

Ideally, I would've much preferred to have a singular NGINX container by itself to handle both SSL termination and reverse proxying on its own, but alas, attempting to leave Skill Weaver as its own independent container without an instance of NGINX appears to have a strange bug where it refuses to fully boot up on its own.
