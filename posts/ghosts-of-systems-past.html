Ghosts of Systems Past

Every good idea leaves behind a ghost, this is ours.

Every software project has two histories. The first ends up in conference papers with architecture diagrams, and polished demos. It's clean, logical, and every decision that was made looks inevitable.

The second history is far messier. It’s made up of abandoned ideas, assumptions that turned out to be wrong, and the quiet realization that debugging one piece of code uncovers three more issues.

The Haunted Bicycle exists to tell the second history of the Skill Weaver project.

The problem we couldn't ignore

I have been an educator for a good chunk of the decade, in a traditional classroom setting and have always taught Data Structures and Algorithms the exact same way and assessed the students the exact same way. Year after year I establish a standard set of skills that I expect my students to master and ask them to solve the same problems. The game pieces change, but rules remain the same.

Every year I see the students struggle for various different reasons. One student understands recursion but gets lost in graphs. Another can implement Dijkstra’s algorithm from memory but can’t explain why it works. A third misses one quiz and suddenly appears to know nothing. Yet we give all of them the same assessment. It felt like I was training the students to pass an exam instead of actually mastering the skills.

The road to Skill Weaver

Skill Weaver did not begin as a software, but as a question. Can the assessment adapt to the student, instead of forcing every student down the same path?

A system doesn't get haunted by accident. It gets haunted by everyone who touched it and left something behind.

Over the next few years the answer to that question changed many times. The idea constantly evolved and nearly disappeared in the endless abyss of my calendar. At one point it almost became someone else’s. Eventually a capstone team brought the first real version to life. The interface, the backend worked and the students could finally take adaptive quizzes.

But unfortunately the mathematics didn’t. That wasn’t a failure but a moment of clarity that this was not a class project anymore but a research problem.

Why the Haunted Bicycle Exists

Most engineering blogs read like press releases where every decision seems obvious and deliberate in hindsight. That’s never how software is actually built.

The Haunted Bicycle is our engineering journal where we’ll revisit abandoned ideas, explain the decisions we regret, document the math that didn’t work, and occasionally celebrate the moments where everything somehow came together.

Every system leaves behind ghosts. This blog is about meeting them again.

Welcome to the Haunted Bicycle. Let’s see where the ride takes us.
