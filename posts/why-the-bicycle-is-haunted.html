Why the Bicycle Is Haunted

On naming a system after something with a mind of its own, and why every complex codebase eventually earns a ghost.

Manju Jan 12, 2026 7 min read
In this post
  1. Naming things is a design decision
  2. The first ghost
  3. Why three characters
  4. What this means for how we write

Every project accumulates a second, unofficial architecture: the one made of workarounds, deprecated flags nobody deleted, and comments that say // don't touch this without saying why. That architecture doesn't show up on any diagram. It shows up in Slack threads at 11pm and in the particular way a senior engineer flinches when you mention a specific file.

We wanted a name for that layer, because pretending it doesn't exist is how it gets worse. Haunting was the word that stuck, and once it stuck, the rest of the metaphor built itself.

Naming things is a design decision

Skill Weaver is an adaptive assessment system. On paper, that means item response theory, ability estimation, and a pipeline that selects the next question a student sees based on everything they've answered so far. In practice, it also means a codebase that has been rewritten twice, a scoring service that three different people have independently declared "basically fine now," and a caching layer whose behavior under load has become a kind of local legend.

None of that is unusual. It's what happens to any system that survives contact with real users for more than a year. What's unusual is writing about it in public, in the order it actually happened, instead of only after it's been cleaned up for a conference talk.

A system doesn't get haunted by accident. It gets haunted by everyone who touched it and left something behind.

The first ghost

The name came from an actual incident. Early in the project, a caching bug caused the adaptive engine to occasionally serve a student the same difficulty tier twice in a row, silently, with no error and no log line pointing at the cause. We spent two days convinced it was a probability issue in the item selection logic before finding a stale key in a cache that nobody remembered adding.

Jesus, only half joking, said the bug had been "riding around on the bicycle the whole time." The name for the postmortem channel became #haunted-bicycle, and it never got renamed. When it came time to name this journal, there wasn't really a debate.

Why three characters

Monster, Spider, and Bicycle aren't mascots bolted on after the fact. They map onto three things every non-trivial system actually has: someone who builds it, logic that governs it, and the thing that results from both. Giving them names makes it easier to talk precisely about where a problem actually lives.

"The bicycle is broken" means something different from "the spider spun this wrong," which is different again from "the monster forgot to handle this case." That precision is the entire point. It's a lot easier to write a fair postmortem when the language separates the system from the person who built it.

What this means for how we write

A few ground rules, mostly for ourselves:

  • We publish debugging stories close to when they happened, including the parts where we were wrong for a while.
  • We name the specific decision or tradeoff, not just the outcome. "We chose X over Y because Z" is more useful than "we improved performance."
  • Research posts on adaptive assessment get held to the same standard as engineering posts: show the reasoning, not just the conclusion.
  • Nobody has to pretend the first version of anything was good.

That's the whole manifesto. The bicycle is haunted. We're writing down what we find as we ride it.
